Mansaf

Description

Mansaf is a traditional Jordanian dish, made from slow-cooked lamb served atop a delicious dirty rice, and drenched in a delightful yoghurt soupy-type-sauce.


I was taught how to cook this divine dish when I was 18 years old by some lovely Palestinian-Jordanian ladies, who helped me master the basics of cooking good Palestinian and Jordanian food, and though I haven't cooked it in over a decade, I remember it fondly.

Mansaf is, in my humble opinion, one of the best Levantine dishes you could ever eat. So get crackin', whoever comes across this page, and make your tastebuds weep with joy.

Ingredients

  • 1kg Laban Jameed broken into pieces - a hard dried yoghurt
    found sold in many Middle Eastern grocery stores
  • 1 1/2 tsp ground allspice
  • 1/2 tsp cardamom
  • 1/4 tsp ground tumeric
  • 3kg lamb shanks or lamb necks
  • 1/4 oil
  • 1/5 cup pine nuts
  • 1/2 cup of slithered almonds
  • 4 cups dried rice - Basmati or Jasmine
  • A sprig or two of parsley for garnishing
  • Khobz - an Arabic flat bread

Steps

  1. Brown the lamb in a heavy pot. Add all the spices and get them just hot enough to release the fragrances. Cover with water and bring to the boil and turn heat down to low-medium.
  2. Allow the lamb to cook at a simmer for about 2 hours until lamb is falling off the bone.
  3. Remove the meat from the pot and place it to one side on a plate, and set about half of the water aside for cooking the rice later. Leave the other half in the pot. As the lamb stock cools, remove the top-most layer of fat and meat-scum with a ladel, the scum and fat can be discarded.
  4. Put the lamb and put it back in the pot with the lamb stock, and add the laban jameed. Slowly bring to the point just before hitting a rolling boil, stirring to prevent the laban from burning on the bottom. Remove the pot from heat and set it aside momentarily.
  5. Take the rice and put it into a bowl, cover with cold water for about 10 minutes. Rinse rice with running cold water until the water pouring off the rice is clear. Drain the cold wet rice.
  6. Add a smidgen of oil in the bottom of another pot, and get it hot. Gradually sprinkle in 1 cup of the cold wet rice into the pan, while stirring, making sure the rice doesn't stick to the bottom. Once rice starts to brown, dump the rest of the rice into the pot, and then pour in the lamb stock you set aside earlier.
  7. Add a few saffron stamens, and bring the rice, uncovered, to a rolling boil. Turn heat down to low, and cover the pot with a lid, and cook until the water has evaporated.
  8. Take a very small frying-pan, and put into it some oil. Get the oil hot, and dump the almonds and pine-nuts into the pan and stir like your life depends on it. You want the nuts to be just starting to brown before you immediately take them off the heat and place them in another container before they can burn. Trust me, it's easy to burn these, and not fun to have to restart the process.
  9. Place the rice in a large round serving dish, as big as you've got. Carefully take pieces of lamb from the pot and carefully place them upon the rice. Take a ladel and just lightly sprinkle some of the lamb-laban soup over the dish, just enough so the rice has a little of the milky goodness, but don't drown it, leave that for your guests to decide how much they want.
  10. Sprinkle the nuts over the dish, and break up some parsley and sprinkle that over it too.
  11. Serve each guest with laban-soup in bowls, and khobz on the side.
  12. Goes really well with a chilli, tomato and lemon salad, and mint-tea as a refreshing dinner drink. And you can't beat eating all of this at iftar in the evening with friends.
