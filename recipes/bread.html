Whole-Grain Bread

Description

Bread made from wheat-grain you've ground yourself, is a special experience. The bread has a lot of flavour, and is quite heavy and dense. You probably won't want to eat this everyday, unless you love heavy breads, but it certainly is something you might want to try at least once in your life.
You can grind wheat with an electric grinder instead, but that just takes the joy out of doing it the old fashioned arm-breaking way. Which would I choose today? Definitely the easier electric way, but I feel like everyone should have to suffer the pain of doing it the old fashioned way first.


I started griding grain by hand at the age of 8. My parents bought a hand-grinder, and got large 10kg bags of grain that they'd dump into massive plastic drums that kept out the mice. I hated eating the bread I made from this grain, because it was tough and heavy, but it was the only bread I had access to, and I made bread this way every day from the age of 8 until I was 16, so got good at it.

People tend to think that making bread is difficult, but it isn't that hard. Just requires know how and some muscle-power.



Quick Note:


I didn't use any measuring items as a kid, as we didn't have measuring cups or scales or teaspoons, so I created it through going off eye and how heavy it felt. I am providing measurements here though, so that you don't have to learn through trial and error.

Ingredients

  • A hand grinder and 2.5 cups of wheat grain
  • A woodstove that has a nice roaring fire going inside its firebox
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 1 tbspn sugar
  • 1 small packet dried yeast or about 7g dried yeast
  • 3 tbsp oil
  • 1 1/2 cups lukewarm water

Steps

  1. Screw your hand-grinder to the edge of a table or a bench. It is removeable, and you can take it back off straight afterwards, or keep it bolted to to the surface if you love this experience and want to keep making bread this way. There is a screw on the grain-grinder that determines how fine the flour coming out will become. Tighten it as much as you can. The less fine the flour, the more chewy the bread will become.
  2. Fill the top of the grain grinder, which is a bit like a funnel, with wheat grains. Place a bowl or another jug underneath the out-put part of the grain-grinder, which will be over the bench or table. Grip the handle that comes off of the grain grinder and turn it clockwise. Keep turning clockwise, until all the grain in the funnel of the grinder has been ground up and deposited into the bowl or jug. You may have to do this two or three times depending on how much grain the funnel can hold at a time.
  3. Take a small container, and put 1/4 of the lukewarm water therein, the sugar, and yeast. Mix and set aside until it froths up and doubles in height.
  4. Put 2 cups of the flour and salt in a bowl and stir, dump the oil in, and then add the yeast-water mixture, and start stirring.
  5. Keep stirring until it becomes a crumbly sort of dough. Now is the time to get your hands in there. You want to make sure there are no dry floury bits, basically you want to abuse the forming dough until it's become elasticised when you pull one part of the dough away from another part of it with the entire ball of dough. You'll know when you've achieved this state when thin stringy bits that stretch 5cm or long form when you pull a bit of dough away.
  6. Rub some oil over the dough, and set it, covered with a damp cloth, on a warm windowsill, on top of your kerosene powered fridge, or on the coldest side of your woodstove, which should be stoked and set to minimum heat at this point.
  7. Take the bowl with the dough once the dough has double in size. Take the dough out of the bowl and slap it down onto a chopping board or bench that you have sprinkled flour upon.
  8. You're going to want to bash that dough up. You need to smack it, squash it, hit it, pound it, and take the outside edges and push it over its opposite side. You want to take all those tiny air bubbles and make them begone. Don't worry, the dough will make some more when you set it aside again soon. You'll want to knead it for about 5-10 minutes.
  9. Put the dough into an oiled oven-pan, sprinkle flour over the dough in the pan, place a dampened cloth over the top of the dough, and put the dough back into the warm place.
  10. Stoke fire in woodstove. Turn heat up to middle. Once dough has risen to double again, place it in the over, and cook. When you put the dough in the oven, be careful not to knock or tap the pan, as you don't want the thousands of air-bubbles inside the dough to escape this time.
  11. Once the dough is cooked, its now called bread. You'll know its done when the crust is hard on top of the bread-dough, and when you tap the top of the bread it makes a hollow sound and not indent at all. You can stick a skewer or knife into the bread to check if it is done, the skewer should come out clean, but the knocking method works just as well in my opinion.
